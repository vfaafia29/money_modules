Expect the Unexpected: Fa'alavelave

An example of a major financial issue in Samoan communities is managing fa’alavelave which can be funerals, weddings, birthdays etc where there is a financial obligation to give money. Similar variations also exist in other Pasifika and Māori communities.

Family and giving are extremely valued in Samoan culture therefore it’s in or nature to want to give back and help out our families/communities in the form of money donations. This beautiful part of our culture can quickly become out of hand when families prioritise this over putting food on the table for their own households.

Fa’alavelave like funerals often come up unexpectedly so families have to find a way to come up with quick cash. This usually means cutting out household spending or borrowing a loan which can quickly become a heavy financial burden especially with loan sharks.

This poses the question how do we develop a money mindset and/or money habits that allow us to contribute to family/cultural practices but not at the expense of our own financial well-being? We can’t control when a fa’alavelave will happen however we can attempt to prepare by putting money aside for a rainy day and demonstrates the importance of having an emergency fund for when unexpected events like a fa'alavelave come up.

For some families this may not be possible as they trying to live from pay day to pay day therefore finding a balance is extremely important even if this means contributing later or in a different way. The most effective ways of dealing with fa’alavelave is through being proactive instead of reactive because it can have many negative flow-on effects.
